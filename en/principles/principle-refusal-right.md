# Principle: Refusal Rights
- Statement: The subject must retain the right to refuse, question, revise, and exit any intervention.
- Rationale: Without real refusal, “help” becomes domination and the first mind becomes abdicable.
- Implications:
  - Refusal rights belong in the constitution layer (immutable commitments).
  - Every threshold permission must include an easy, non-punitive refusal path.
- Tensions & tradeoffs: Strong refusal rights may reduce efficiency and perceived “helpfulness.”
- Anti-patterns: Dark patterns that punish refusal; hiding the refusal path; making refusal technically possible but practically costly.
